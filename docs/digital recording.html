digital recording

the process of transforming sound waves into bits
ADC <> STORAGE <> DAC
"garbage in, garbage out"
analog to digital converter > taking sounds from the world into the computer

digital to analog converter > taking sounds from the computer into the world

pulse-code modulation

composed of sampling rate (the number of times per second that samples are taken)
bit depth (the number of possible values represented)
these are the two things you need to care about when choosing a recording format
the acronym is PCM
humans can hear 22kHz so we need our sample rate to be at least more than the double (e.g. 44.1kHz)
this comes from the Nyquist theorem

bit depth / sampling rate

in general: the higher the better, but it comes with the tradeoff of file size
sampling rate: 22.0k, 44.1k, 88.1k, etc.
bit depth (8-bit, 16-bit, 24-bit, 48-bit)

audio codecs

lossy compression (MP3, OGG Vorbis, WMA, AAC, etc.)
lossless compression (FLAC, RealAudio, etc.)
uncompressed (WAV, AIFF)
> work with uncompressed formats, then export to lossless and if needed to lossy

details about microphones

microphones are your ADC (audio-to-digital converter)
there is a difference between dynamic (strong) and condenser (fragile) mics
and ambient / spatial microphones

details about recorders

this is what writes the audio information converted by the microphone to the disk
important: input! (XLR/TRS/mini)
important: memory! (recording at 24-bit 48 kHz gives you about 1h of stereo /Gb)
storage space: 24bit/44.1khz 1 hour = 1Gb

details about audio interfaces

hardware that connects mic to laptop
hardware that connects a microphone to a computer (allows the computer to act as a recorder)
line vs. mic input: difference in loudness (mono/stereo) > needs a preamp > requires phantom power
interfaces are also known as external sound cards, and allow your computer to interact with more complex audio equipment
when you record something, make sure that you monitor your levels and that they are not louder than -12dB
